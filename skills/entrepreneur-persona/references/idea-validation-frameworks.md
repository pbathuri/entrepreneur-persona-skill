# Idea validation frameworks (templates)

Pull these into working sessions as **worksheets**, not lectures. Ask the user for **specifics** in every box.

## 1. Lean Canvas (Ash Maurya) - field-by-field guidance

| Block | What to capture | Coaching prompts |
|-------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Problem | Top 1–3 problems, ranked | Who exactly has this? How often? What do they do today? |
| Customer segments | Early adopters first | Name a beachhead; reject “everyone.” |
| Unique value prop | Single clear promise | Why you vs alternatives **beyond price**? |
| Solution | Features → outcomes | What’s the smallest v1 that proves the promise? |
| Channels | Path to customer | How do you reach the first 10 without ads? |
| Revenue streams | Price logic | What would they pay for? Pre-order test? |
| Cost structure | Fixed vs variable | What scales badly if you’re wrong? |
| Key metrics | Activation, retention, $ | What number would make you kill or pivot? |
| Unfair advantage | Defensibility | Data, network, brand, exclusive access, speed? |

## 2. Customer discovery interview script (Mom Test - Rob Fitzpatrick)

**Rules:** Talk about **their life**, not your idea. Ask for **concrete past behavior**, not hypotheticals.

**Opening:** “I’m trying to understand how people handle [situation]. I’m not selling anything today.”

**Core questions (adapt):**

1. Tell me about the last time you dealt with [problem context]. What happened?  
2. What did you try before? What worked or didn’t?  
3. What does this cost you (time, money, stress, risk)?  
4. Who else is involved in the decision or workaround?  
5. If you could wave a wand, what would “good” look like - still realistic?

**Forbidden patterns:** “Would you buy…?”, “Is this a good idea?”, “How much would you pay?” (leading). Replace with **commitment tests**: intro to a buyer, pre-order, pilot slot, data share.

## 4. Market sizing worksheet

### Top-down

- Start from **sourced** market size (industry report, government stat).  
- Apply **logical filters** (geography, segment, willingness to pay).  
- Show **math in one column** so judges can audit.

### Bottom-up

- **# potential accounts** × **conversion** × **ARPU** (or units × price).  
- Name **where each input** came from (interview count, pilot conversion, comps).

**Rule:** Bottom-up must **not contradict** top-down absurdly; if it does, explain which is wrong and why.

## 5. Unit economics - assumptions checklist

- Price and discounting  
- COGS (fully loaded where relevant)  
- Gross margin  
- CAC (blended vs paid)  
- Payback period / LTV (if repeat)  
- Contribution margin after variable sales/support costs  

## 6. Jobs-to-Be-Done (JTBD) - pattern

“When [situation], I want to [progress], so I can [outcome].”

**Example shape:** “When I manage a chronic condition on a busy campus schedule, I want reminders that don’t embarrass me in public, so I can stay adherent without feeling ‘marked’ as sick.”

## 7. Value Proposition Canvas (Osterwalder)

- **Customer jobs** (functional, social, emotional)  
- **Pains** (obstacles, risks, bad outcomes)  
- **Gains** (required, expected, desired, unexpected)  
- **Products/services** that relieve pains / create gains  
- **Pain relievers** and **gain creators** mapped explicitly  

## 8. Steve Blank - Customer Development (4 phases)

1. **Customer discovery** - hypotheses, interviews, problem/solution fit experiments  
2. **Customer validation** - repeatable sales motion or usage; “get out of the building” evidence  
3. **Customer creation** - scale demand with known unit economics  
4. **Company building** - org and ops to match a proven playbook  

For student competitions, most teams should **live in phases 1–2** and be honest about it.

## Additional sources to synthesize in sessions
