# Pitch patterns (competition-tested)

Patterns observed in strong student competition pitches, enriched with widely used entrepreneurship frameworks. Use these when coaching decks, scripts, and Q&A.

## Pattern: The Personal Hook

- Open with a personal story that connects the founder to the problem. Judges remember people and stakes before they remember slides.
- Examples of shape (not to copy verbatim): a family moment that exposed friction; a firsthand failure of the status quo; a specific day the problem became unavoidable.
- Framework: Nancy Duarte’s *Resonate* - contrast **what is** (painful today) with **what could be** (relief, dignity, speed, access).

## Pattern: Problem–Solution Bridge

- Name the problem with a **specific** statistic or concrete behavior (who, how often, cost of the workaround).
- Show why **current alternatives** fail (bulk, stigma, price, trust, time, fragmentation).
- Bridge with: “That’s why we built [X] - it helps [customer] do [job] without [failure mode].”

## Pattern: TAM / SAM / SOM Funnel

- **TAM**: Broad universe (cite defensible sources: government data, industry reports, academic studies).
- **SAM**: Who you can realistically serve with your model, geography, and regulatory constraints.
- **SOM**: Beachhead you can win in 12–24 months with your actual channels.
- Never confuse “big number” with “credible number.” Show the funnel logic, not just three circles.

## Pattern: Validation Evidence Hierarchy (strongest to weakest)

1. Revenue / paying customers  
2. Signed contracts / LOIs with clear terms  
3. Waitlist, pre-orders, deposits  
4. Structured interviews / surveys with disclosed sample and method  
5. Expert or distribution partner validation (with specifics)  
6. Anecdotes (“people love it”) without method  

Coach founders to **climb the ladder** with the next cheapest test.

## Pattern: Use of Funds Specificity

- Break down by category with **dollar amounts** and **percentages** (product, marketing, inventory, legal/IP, people).
- Tie each line to a **milestone** (e.g., “$6k → working prototype v2 with manufacturable CAD”).
- Avoid “miscellaneous,” “general ops,” or opaque buckets.

## Pattern: Conservative Credibility

- Prefer floor assumptions with explicit “if we only capture X% of Y” logic over hockey sticks.
- State what you **do not know yet** and the experiment that will reduce uncertainty.
- Language shape: “We’re intentionally conservative because judges penalize fantasy curves.”

## Pattern: Growth Phases

- **Phase 1 - Beachhead**: narrow segment, learn distribution, tighten unit economics, earn testimonials.  
- **Phase 2 - Expansion**: adjacent segments, partnerships, new geography, channel tests.  
- **Phase 3 - Scale**: repeatable playbook, capital-efficient growth, moats that aren’t “we’re cheaper.”

## Enrichment: frameworks to cite in coaching (apply, don’t lecture)

- **Guy Kawasaki - 10/20/30**: ~10 slides, ~20 minutes, ~30pt font; one idea per slide.  
- **Reid Hoffman - *Blitzscaling***: when speed matters more than efficiency; only after product–market fit signals.  
- **Eric Ries - *The Lean Startup***: build–measure–learn; smallest experiment that falsifies a belief.  
- **Alex Osterwalder - Business Model Canvas**: coherent story across customer, value, channels, revenue, cost.  
- **Geoffrey Moore - *Crossing the Chasm***: beachhead niche before “everyone.”  
- **Bill Aulet - *Disciplined Entrepreneurship***: staged de-risking from who → what → how → how much.  
- **Y Combinator**: make something people want; talk to users; launch uncomfortably early.  
- **Peter Thiel - *Zero to One***: secrets (non-consensus truths); avoid undifferentiated competition.  
- **Paul Graham**: do things that don’t scale; default alive; users over prestige.
