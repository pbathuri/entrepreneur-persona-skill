# SnackBox — Healthy Snacks for College Students

## Section 1: Summary

**S1 — Product/service + pain:** SnackBox delivers curated healthy snack boxes to college dorm rooms on a weekly subscription basis. College students living in dorms without kitchens rely on vending machines, fast food, and convenience stores for between-meal nutrition, leading to poor dietary habits, energy crashes, and weight gain during their first year.

**S2 — Uniqueness:** Unlike generic snack boxes (SnackNation, Graze), SnackBox is designed around campus schedules and dorm logistics — delivery to mailrooms on Sunday evenings, portion-sized for backpack carry, and priced at $8.99/week (less than two vending runs).

**S3 — Target customer:** First- and second-year students at Big Ten universities living in on-campus housing without kitchen access, ages 18–20, who express interest in health/wellness but lack time and access to prepare meals.

**S4 — Founder motivation:** As a sophomore who gained 15 pounds freshman year eating vending machine food three times a week, I lived this problem. My residence hall had 400 students and zero healthy grab-and-go options after 8pm.

## Section 2: Industry/Market

**I1 — Competitive landscape:**
- Direct: SnackNation (office-focused, $150+/mo minimum), Graze (UK-heavy, no campus distribution)
- Indirect: Campus convenience stores, vending machines, campus dining meal plans
- Substitute/DIY: Grocery store runs, parents mailing care packages

**I2 — UVP vs alternatives:**

| Alternative | Their strength | Gap we exploit |
|-------------|----------------|-----------------|
| SnackNation | Established brand, variety | Office-priced, no dorm delivery, minimum too high for students |
| Vending machines | Ubiquitous, instant | 90% junk food, $2-3 per item adds up, no variety |
| Campus dining | Included in meal plan | Closes at 9pm, limited healthy grab-and-go |

**I3 — Validation evidence:** Surveyed 87 students across 3 residence halls (paper survey, February 2026). 71% said they snack 2+ times daily; 64% said they'd pay $8-10/week for a healthy option delivered to their mailroom. Conducted 12 one-on-one interviews following Mom Test principles. 8 of 12 said their biggest barrier to healthy eating was access after dining hall hours.

**I4 — Industry challenges:** Perishability of some snack items (mitigated by selecting shelf-stable options for v1); campus vendor approval processes (already in conversation with Penn State housing office).

## Section 3: Marketing

**M1A — Macro customer:** 18-20 year old college students, on-campus residents, Big Ten universities, household income $50k+ (proxy: attending 4-year university).

**M1B — Micro/psychographic:** Health-curious but not health-obsessed. Uses Instagram and TikTok. Influenced by peer behavior. Values convenience over perfection. Budget-conscious but willing to spend on small weekly treats.

**M2 — Market sizing:**
- TAM: 2.1M students living in on-campus housing at US 4-year universities (NCES 2024). At $8.99/week × 32 weeks = $287.68/year × 2.1M = $604M.
- SAM: Big Ten conference schools, ~180,000 on-campus residents. $287.68 × 180,000 = $51.8M.
- SOM (12-24 months): Penn State and Ohio State only, target 2% penetration of 28,000 on-campus students = 560 subscribers × $287.68 = $161K year 1 revenue.

**M3 — GTM channels:**
1. Residence hall ambassador program (free box for referrals)
2. Tabling at move-in weekend and student org fairs
3. Instagram micro-influencers on each campus (trade for product)

**M4 — Core message:** "Healthy snacking, delivered to your dorm — cheaper than the vending machine."

**M5 — Marketing challenges:** Student turnover every year requires re-acquisition each fall (mitigated by ambassador program continuity and targeting freshmen at orientation).

## Section 4: Operations

**O1 — Technology + development plan:** Shopify subscription storefront with campus-specific SKUs. No custom tech needed for v1. Order management via Shopify + ShipStation.

**O2 — Manufacturing/distribution:** Source shelf-stable snacks wholesale from UNFI and local distributors. Assemble boxes in rented commercial kitchen space near campus ($200/month). Deliver via student drivers to campus mailrooms on Sunday evenings.

**O3 — Operational challenges:** Mailroom capacity during peak periods (mitigated by staggering delivery windows and pre-coordinating with housing staff); maintaining snack variety without spoilage (mitigated by 2-week inventory rotation and shelf-stable focus).

## Section 5: Financial

**F1 — Average price per unit/service:** $8.99/week per subscription box.

**F2 — Cost per unit/service (COGS):** $4.20 per box (snacks $3.10 wholesale, packaging $0.60, delivery labor $0.50).

**F3 — Year 1 & Year 2 projections:**
- Y1: 560 subscribers average × 32 weeks = 17,920 boxes. Revenue: $161,043. Assumptions: 2% penetration at 2 campuses, 80% retention through academic year.
- Y2: 1,400 subscribers × 32 weeks = 44,800 boxes. Revenue: $402,752. Assumptions: expand to 4 Big Ten campuses, 3.5% penetration, ambassador program drives 60% of new sign-ups.

**F4 — Gross profit:**
Y1: 17,920 units × ($8.99 − $4.20) = 17,920 × $4.79 = $85,837 gross profit (53% margin).
Y2: 44,800 units × ($8.99 − $4.20) = 44,800 × $4.79 = $214,592 gross profit.

**F5 — Financial risks:** Wholesale cost increases (mitigated by multi-supplier relationships); lower-than-projected retention (mitigated by mid-semester survey and box customization in semester 2); seasonality — zero revenue during summer and breaks (mitigated by 32-week model assumption, not 52).
